## Part B - Survey Evaluation:

Identify and describe survey features:

```
1. Sample type:
the General Social Survey on Giving, Volunteering and Participating (GSS GVP) uses a probability-based, cross-sectional sample survey design. Data are collected from individuals at one point in time to represent the Canadian population aged 15 and over living in private households.

2. Sample size:
a field sample of approximately 50,000 units was used. About 40,000 households were invited to participate, and approximately 24,000 completed questionnaires were expected.

3. Target population :
the target population includes all persons aged 15 years and older living in private households in the ten provinces of Canada. Full-time residents of institutions (living there more than six months) are excluded.

4. Sampling frame: 
the sampling frame combines landline and cellular telephone numbers from the Census and administrative sources with Statistics Canadaâ€™s dwelling frame. Records consist of groups of telephone numbers associated with the same address, or a single number if no address link was available.

5. Survey mode :
data were collected directly from respondents using either an electronic self-completed questionnaire or computer-assisted telephone interviewing . Proxy responses were not permitted, and respondents could complete the survey in English or French.

6. Timeline:
Data collection took place from September 4, 2018 to December 28, 2018. The reference period for most questions was the 12 months preceding the interview date. The data were released on January 26, 2021.

7. Response rate:  
the overall response rate for the 2018 GSS GVP was 41.9%.

8. Weights :
survey weights were created so that each respondent represents multiple individuals in the population. The main person-level weight is WGHT_PER. Adjustments were made for non-response and for the rejective sampling design. Bootstrap weights were also created to support variance estimation.

9. Data processing:  
data processing followed Statistics Canadaâ€™s standardized SSPE processing framework. Automated and manual edits were applied throughout data collection and procesing to ensure logical consistency, correct flows and valid ranges.

10. Cleaning, imputation, etc.  
Missing data were handled primarily through donor imputation, where responses from similar respondents were used to fill in missing values. Mean imputation was used when donor imputation was not possible. Income variables were largely obtained through linkage to tax records, with remaining missing values imputed.

11. Sources of error  
sources of error include sampling error, non-response erro, coverage error, response error, and processing error. Sampling variability was estimated using bootstrap methods. Non-sampling errors were addresed through survey design, weighting and quality control procedures.

12. Limitations, known biases, etc.  
key limitations include exclusion of people living in institutions and households without telephone access. Despite weighting adjustmens, non-response bias may still exist. Mode effects may also occur due to differences between telephone and online responses.
```
